Methodology & sources

This chart races New York City's foreign-born population by country of birth from 1900 to 2023, and fades each wave as it recedes. Here is exactly where the data comes from, how the fade is defined and what it can and cannot show.

What the chart shows

Each bar is the number of foreign-born New Yorkers born in a given country at each decennial census — the immigrant, or first, generation. It does not include their US-born children or grandchildren. The chart shows the largest groups at any moment (the top dozen), so a country appears when it grows large enough and drops off when it is overtaken.

What the fade means — and what it doesn't. The fade is the heart of this chart, so be clear on what it is. The U.S. census can identify immigrants and (historically) their children by nation, but it cannot trace a group by country of birth past the second generation — by the third generation, descendants are simply recorded as American-born, with no foreign birthplace. So a wave that stops being replenished by new arrivals does two things at once: it shrinks (its immigrants age and die) and it disappears from the birthplace count (its grandchildren are no longer counted under that nation). We encode this by fading a bar once it is past its peak decade, in proportion to how far it has fallen from that peak. The fade is therefore a visual proxy for generational succession, not a measured count of third- or fourth-generation people — that figure does not exist in the historical data. A faded Italian or Irish bar does not mean those New Yorkers vanished; it means they became second-, third- and fourth-generation Americans whom the census no longer counts as Italy- or Ireland-born.

The chart places the 2011 ACS figures in the 2010 column.
  • 1900 and 1910: U.S. Census Bureau, 1910 Census, Volume 1, "Country of Birth of the Foreign-Born Population," Table 29 (distribution of the foreign-born population by principal countries of birth, cities of 250,000+, 1910 and 1900), New York city. census.gov
  • 1920 and 1930: U.S. Census Bureau, 1930 Census, Volume II (Population, General Report), Chapter 5, Table 9 ("Country of birth of the foreign-born population, by sex, for cities of 100,000 or more: 1930 and 1920"), New York, N.Y. census.gov The 1930 city total derived from this table (2,358,686) matches the published census total.
  • 1940, 1950, 1960: not yet transcribed from primary sources. These cells are left blank and the bars interpolate across them. For the large European groups the real trend across this span is a steady decline, so the interpolation understates none of the major peaks (which fall in 1900–1930) but should not be read as exact mid-century figures.
  • 2020: U.S. Census Bureau, American Community Survey 2022 5-year estimates, table B05006 ("Place of Birth for the Foreign-Born Population"), New York city. China combines the mainland, Hong Kong and Taiwan to match the City Planning definition used for 1970–2011. data.census.gov
  • 2023: NYC Department of City Planning, The Newest New Yorkers 2026, Chapter 2, "Top 20 Foreign-born Groups" (based on the 2023 American Community Survey). nyc.gov The report ranks the 20 largest groups; the nine chart countries that fall outside its top 20 (Germany, Ireland, Austria, Hungary, Poland, Romania, Greece, Sweden and Cuba) are taken from the 2023 ACS 1-year table B05006, summed across the five boroughs, on the same 2023 basis. China again combines the mainland, Hong Kong and Taiwan. In 2023 China (397,249) overtook the Dominican Republic (389,779) as the largest group, Bangladesh rose to seventh, and Ghana became the first African country in the city's top 20 in the post-1965 era.
  • City foreign-born totals cross-checked against NYC City Planning's "Foreign-born Population, New York City & Boroughs, 1900–2010."

Every filled figure is sourced to the volumes above. Where a decade's number for a country could not be reliably sourced, that cell is left blank rather than estimated, and the bar interpolates across the gap. The 2020 column uses the ACS 2022 5-year estimate (centered on 2020); the 2023 column uses the 2023 ACS as published in The Newest New Yorkers 2026. The final step spans three years (2020–2023) rather than a full decade, and animates over the same interval on the clock.

Definitions and boundary changes

  • Foreign-born = people living in New York City who were born outside the United States and its territories. This is the census definition; it includes naturalized citizens, lawful residents, and others.
  • Poland did not exist as an independent state at the 1900–1910 censuses; Poles were counted under Russia, Austria and Germany. Poland appears as its own bar once the census recorded it as a country of birth.
  • Russian Empire / U.S.S.R. is one continuous bar spanning the Russian Empire → the Soviet Union → present-day Russia, because the census recorded birthplace as the state of the day. This label matters: before about 1920 the census counted everyone from this territory simply as “Russia,” and the great majority of these New Yorkers were Ashkenazi Jewish emigrants from the Pale of Settlement — land that is now Ukraine, Belarus, Lithuania, eastern Poland and Moldova — not ethnic Russians. So the large early bar reflects the Eastern European Jewish migration, not migration from Russia proper. Ukraine breaks out separately only from 2000, when the census reported the former Soviet republics individually; Lithuania and Latvia appear in the 1930 census (after independence) but are small and not drawn as their own bars here.
  • Austria in 1900–1910 is really Austria-Hungary — the bar includes Czechs, Slovaks, Poles, Croats, Slovenes and others from across the empire, not only people from present-day Austria. The empire's 1918 break-up scattered these births among successor states, which is why the Austria and Hungary counts shift sharply around 1920.
  • Czechoslovakia and Yugoslavia are shown under those names for the years the census used them; their bars end when the census stopped reporting them.
  • Some early-century foreign-born counts (notably 1920, 1930 and 1950 in certain census tables) were tabulated for the white foreign-born only; for New York City in this era the difference is small, but it is noted here for transparency.

What is interpolated

A census is taken once a decade. Between censuses, each country's value is linearly interpolated so the bars move continuously. The numbers are exact only at each data year (when the year reads 1900, 1910, … 2020, and the final 2023). A wave that the census had not yet recorded grows in from zero; a bar with no further data ebbs toward zero.

Limitations

  • This is a chart of birthplace, not ancestry or ethnicity. It tracks immigrants, not descendants. The companion chart, "Who lives here," shows the city by race and ethnicity, which captures all generations.
  • The fade is illustrative. Two different waves past their peaks are not necessarily at the same "generational" stage; the fade reflects decline from peak, which is a reasonable but imperfect stand-in.
  • Country categories and borders shifted enormously over 120 years; cross-century comparisons for any single country should be read as broadly indicative.
